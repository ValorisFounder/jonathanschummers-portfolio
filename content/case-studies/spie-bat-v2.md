## Headline

Cut manual data entry on construction sites by redesigning the activity report through 16 field workshops

## Subtitle

Co-designed Spie Batignolles' new digital activity report alongside an ERP rollout, using design thinking to harmonize processes across procurement, equipment tracking, and project monitoring without imposing a top-down framework on field crews.

## Context & Objectives

### Problem

Spie Batignolles was rolling out a new ERP and used the opportunity to harmonize its activity report across multiple business modules: Personnel and Equipment Tracking, Procurement, and Project Monitoring. Their previous tools required heavy manual data entry, slowed project monitoring, and weren't aligned across business units. The hard part wasn't the software: a top-down redesign would fail on site. Construction crews have their own way of working, and any tool that ignores that gets bypassed.

### Target audience

Site managers, foremen, equipment supervisors, and procurement officers across multiple Spie Batignolles construction projects: the people filling in activity reports daily.

### My role

Junior UX/UI Designer alongside a Lead UX Designer and another junior, working with around ten people on the ERP implementation team. My first long-term client assignment: heavy mentorship from the Lead, ownership of a large number of screens, and a deliberate progression from executor to workshop facilitator over the 9 months.

## How I solved this problem

### 1. Mapped the current activity report process module by module

For each module (Personnel, Equipment, Procurement, Project Monitoring), we ran 12 user interviews with operational staff, mapped 4 stakeholder relationships, and built 4 "as-is" user journeys. The goal: surface the real workflows on construction sites before redesigning anything, so the harmonized report would reflect field reality rather than head-office assumptions.

### 2. Co-designed the new report with users in 16 workshops

Rather than presenting a finished design, we ran 16 workshops using projective scenarios and low-fidelity mockups. Each module had its own series of sessions where field staff sketched and validated workflows alongside the ERP team. This kept the redesign grounded in operational needs and built buy-in: the people who'd use the tool helped shape it.

### 3. Tested the harmonized flows with 12 user tests

Iterated on the design through 12 user tests focused on the most repeated tasks: filling in personnel time, logging equipment use, raising procurement requests, and tracking project progress. Each iteration cut more friction from the data-entry path, and validated that a single harmonized template could work across all four modules.

### 4. Designed pixel-perfect UI across all modules

Translated the validated wireframes into production-ready screens. With the high screen volume across multiple modules, this is where I made the largest jump in UI/visual craft, a benefit of the long timeline and the Lead's direct mentorship.

### 5. Stepped up as a workshop facilitator

By the end of the assignment, I was leading workshops on lower-stakes modules, a step up from the start, where I was hesitant to speak in front of the client. The transition from executor to facilitator was a deliberate part of the assignment plan and one of the strongest personal outcomes of the project.

## What we delivered

- A harmonized digital activity report across Personnel, Equipment, Procurement, and Project Monitoring modules
- Auto-fill functionality reducing manual data entry on the most repeated tasks
- Centralized procurement workflows cutting request processing time
- 16 co-design workshops, 12 user interviews, 4 stakeholder maps, 4 user journeys, 12 user tests: a complete research and design archive owned by the client team
- A redesign rooted in field reality, built bottom-up with the people who use the tool every day
